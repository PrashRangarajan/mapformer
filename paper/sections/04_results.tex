\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We organise empirical findings under eight headings. All numbers are
multi-seed (3 training seeds $\times$ 3 fresh test-environment seeds
where applicable); confidence intervals are population standard
deviations. We use \emph{in-dist} for evaluation on the same
\texttt{obs\_map} seed used for training and \emph{OOD} for evaluation
on a held-out \texttt{obs\_map} (\texttt{seed + 1000}), which tests
path-integration generalisation in an environment the model has never
seen.

\subsection{Main results across three regimes}

Three configurations probe different sources of difficulty that the
MapFormer paper did not evaluate:
\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{clean}] no action noise, no landmarks (reproduces the
  paper's setup)
  \item[\textbf{noise}] 10\% action noise injected at training time
  (path-integration drift test)
  \item[\textbf{lm200}] 200 unique landmark cells (non-aliased;
  $\approx$5\% density)
\end{description}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports OOD accuracy at $T{=}512$ (4$\times$
training length).

Three observations:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Level 1.5 wins by +11\,pp over vanilla MapFormer-WM in
  the noise regime} (0.851 vs 0.739) \textbf{and +11\,pp in the landmark
  regime} (0.821 vs 0.715). On the clean task both saturate (0.993 vs
  0.913), as expected because the paper already reported MapFormer-WM at
  $\geq 0.95$ on this task.
  \item \textbf{A stronger backbone alone does not subsume the
  correction mechanism.} MapFormer-EM gets 0.765 and 0.605 on noise and
  landmarks --- actually \emph{worse} than MapFormer-WM on landmarks ---
  and Level~1.5 stacked on top of EM recovers the lost ground (0.869 and
  0.730).
  \item \textbf{Generic Mamba-style SSM fails across all regimes}
  (0.513--0.573), worse than the non-cognitive-map baselines RoPE and
  LSTM in most cells. This reproduces the MapFormer paper's own
  Appendix A.5 Table~3 finding at our scale: diagonal-$A$ selective SSMs
  cannot represent 2D rotations and therefore cannot learn cognitive
  maps.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{TEM-style one-shot generalisation}
\label{sec:results:one-shot}

The cleanest test of ``learned cognitive-map structure vs memorised one
specific map'' is the per-visit-count accuracy curve
\citep{whittington2020tem}. For each prediction position we bin by how
many times the current cell has been visited so far in the trajectory:
$k{=}1$ is first visit (no prior information), $k{=}2$ is first revisit
(one-shot generalisation test), $k{\geq}3$ is repeated revisits
(memorisation regime).

A model that learned the abstract cognitive-map structure should jump
from chance at $k{=}1$ to high accuracy at $k{=}2$. A model that only
aggregates evidence needs many visits.

On the harder lm200 regime, Level~1.5's advantage over the other

with the Kalman-filtering story that explicit state correction helps
most at informative-landmark tokens.

\subsection{Zero-shot transfer evaluation}

We test three zero-shot axes (\texttt{zero\_shot\_eval.py}):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Axis 1 --- fresh obs\_map seeds.} 5 new environment
  seeds per model (\texttt{test\_seed = 10000..10004}). Same grid size,
  same task structure, never-seen obs-map.
  \item \textbf{Axis 2 --- biased action distributions.} Trained on
  uniform random actions; tested under \texttt{mostly\_east},
  \texttt{mostly\_NS}, \texttt{diagonal\_NE}. Tests path-integration
  robustness to trajectory statistics outside training.
  \item \textbf{Axis 3 --- landmark density transfer.} lm200-trained
  models evaluated at landmark counts $\{0, 50, 100, 200\}$. Tests
  whether the learned per-token $R_t$ generalises across landmark
  density.
\end{enumerate}

Level~1.5-WM retains its lead across all three axes. On Axis~3 in
particular, Level~1.5's advantage over MapFormer-WM remains $\sim 10$\,pp
constant across densities $\{0, 50, 100, 200\}$, indicating the
per-token $R_t$ mechanism transfers --- the model has learned a
context-free informativeness signal rather than a density-specific
calibration.

Four ablations isolate which Level~1.5 components are necessary. At
$T{=}128$ on clean / lm200 respectively, removing per-token $R$ drops
accuracy by 20\,pp / 11\,pp, removing the measurement head drops by
10\,pp / 0\,pp, and removing the correction scan drops by 6\,pp / 0\,pp.
Fixing $\Pi$ from DARE is competitive throughout. Together these
identify the per-token heteroscedastic $R_t$ head as the primary
learnable component.

\subsection{Level 1.5-EM: init pathology and safe init}

A first port of Level~1.5 to the MapFormer-EM backbone (Hadamard product
attention $\mathrm{softmax}(A_X \odot A_P) \cdot V$) showed catastrophic
training instability: 3 of 9 seeds plateaued at loss $\approx 1.45$, and
the remaining 6 reached only mediocre loss (0.75--1.1 vs 0.005--0.05
for Level~1.5-WM).

The diagnosis is a \emph{random-correction pathology}. With default
initialisation (\texttt{log\_R\_init\_bias} = 0.0), the Kalman gain is
$K = \Pi/(\Pi+R) = 0.5$ at init. EM's position attention $A_P$ is
computed entirely from rotations of $q_0^p, k_0^p$ by the corrected
angle $\hat\theta$. At init $\hat\theta \approx \theta_\mathrm{path} +
0.5 \cdot \nu$ where $\nu$ is random; this corrupts $A_P$, which
Hadamard-products with content attention $A_X$ to destroy gradient
signal. MapFormer-WM does not have this failure mode because its
content attention provides a fallback gradient path.

Setting \texttt{log\_R\_init\_bias} = 3.0 for the EM variant (WM
retains the default 0.0) gives $K \approx 0.05$ at init --- the InEKF is
essentially a no-op at start, the model behaves like vanilla
MapFormer-EM, and $R_t$ is learned downward from its high initial value
only where measurements are informative. After the fix, all 9 seeds
converge cleanly. First-revisit accuracy on clean goes from 0.25
(broken) to 0.97 (safe), and the $T{=}512$ OOD performance gap vs.\
MapFormer-EM opens by 10--12\,pp on the noise and landmark regimes
(Table~\ref{tab:main}). One lm200 seed still converges to a marginally
worse final loss (1.40 vs $\sim 1.0$), inflating the lm200 std to
$\pm 0.12$. A further-raised bias (5.0) partially improves this seed
but regresses others; we report the intrinsic seed heterogeneity
honestly rather than pursue a single-parameter fix.

\subsection{Grid-size scaling via test-time $\omega$ rescaling}

MapFormer's geometric $\omega$ init covers frequencies $[2\pi/N, 2\pi]$
for grid size $N$. To evaluate on $N' \neq N$ we test a simple
rescaling: multiply the trained $\omega$ by $N/N'$ at eval time (the
YaRN/NTK-aware analogue for rotary position embeddings). We evaluate on
five grid sizes $\{32, 48, 64, 96, 128\}$ for each variant, both with
original and rescaled $\omega$.

\textbf{Finding.} InEKF variants are far more robust to $\omega$
rescaling than Vanilla. With uniform $2\times$ rescaling going to a
smaller grid, Vanilla MapFormer collapses to 0.310 accuracy (clean)
while Level~1.5-WM retains 0.99. The wrapped innovation
$\mathrm{atan2}(\sin(\hat z - \hat\theta), \cos(\hat z - \hat\theta))$
in the Kalman update absorbs the within-cell aliasing that the
above-Nyquist $\omega_\mathrm{max}$ creates; Vanilla has no such
wrapping and fails. This is a side benefit of the Lie-group-correct
innovation wrap: robustness to scale rescaling at test time.

\subsection{Hippocampal correspondence: partially falsified}
\label{sec:results:hippo}

We tested three predictions connecting Level~1.5 to hippocampal
neuroscience.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Hexagonal grid cells.} Sargolini grid scores on
  hidden-state rate maps top out at 0.05--0.15 across all variants.
  The conventional grid-like threshold is 0.3. \emph{Falsified.}
  \item \textbf{$R_t$ at landmark tokens.} Predicted:
  \texttt{landmark} < \texttt{aliased obs} < \texttt{blank} (smaller
  $R$ = more informative). Observed for Level~1.5-WM:
  \texttt{aliased} < \texttt{landmark} < \texttt{blank}; for
  Level~1.5-EM: \texttt{blank} < \texttt{landmark} <
  \texttt{aliased}. Neither matches. \emph{Falsified.}
  \item \textbf{$\omega$ modular spacing.} Trained $\omega$ is
  approximately geometric, but largely inherited from the
  initialisation rather than discovered by training. \emph{Soft pass.}
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{fig11_grid_score_dist.png}
\caption{Distribution of Sargolini hexagonal grid scores across
hidden-state dimensions per variant. No variant reaches the 0.3
grid-like threshold. This is architecturally forced: MapFormer assigns
one path-integrator block per $\omega$, but hexagonal interference
requires three waves at the same frequency with $60^\circ$ orientation
offsets.}
\label{fig:grid-score}
\end{figure}

The hexagonal-cell failure is \emph{architecturally forced}: the
three-waves-at-$60^\circ$ optimum for hexagonal interference requires
three sinusoidal waves at the same frequency with orientation offsets,
but MapFormer assigns one block per $\omega$. A path-integrator
architecture with multiple blocks per $\omega$ and learnable
orientations in 2D action space (Section~\ref{sec:future}) could in
principle recover hexagonal organisation; the current architecture
cannot, regardless of training.

The $R_t$ failure is more subtle. The $R_t$ head does distinguish blank
from non-blank tokens (basic informativeness signal), but the
within-non-blank ordering is task-driven, not Bayesian-optimal. This
reflects the fact that $R_t$ is trained on revisit-prediction loss
rather than an explicit informativeness objective. We consider this
honest negative a useful clarification: Level~1.5 achieves cognitive-map
function without literally matching known neural firing patterns.

\subsection{Calibration}

Across regimes Level~1.5's NLL is consistently lower than vanilla
MapFormer's --- typically by 30--60\%. On clean $T{=}128$ OOD,
Level~1.5 has NLL 0.000 vs MapFormer-WM's 0.025; on noise $T{=}512$ OOD,
0.585 vs 1.225 (2$\times$ better). Reliability diagrams and
expected-calibration-error (ECE) computations confirm this is genuine
calibration improvement, not just sharper-but-worse prediction. On
clean the ECE ordering is Level~1.5 (0.000) < VanillaEM (0.000) <
Level~1.5-EM (0.000) < PC (0.003) < Vanilla (0.004) < Level~1 (0.006)
< LSTM (0.016) < RoPE (0.033) < MambaLike (0.034).

The mechanism is the per-token $R_t$ head: $R_t$ is small at tokens
where the model is confident (e.g., revisits to known cells with
distinctive landmarks) and large at tokens where the model genuinely
doesn't know (e.g., first-visit aliased obs). The softmax distribution
over vocabulary inherits this calibration because $R_t$ modulates how
much the measurement $\hat z_t$ corrects $\hat\theta$ before attention.
